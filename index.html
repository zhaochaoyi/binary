Preference-Based Thresholds

These thresholds are nested in a fixed order — as the focused-asset PoS \(p^*\) rises, the ranking moves from the portfolio dominating to the focused asset dominating: \(p^*_{\mathrm{FSD},L}\le p^*_{\mathrm{SSD},L}\le p^*_{\log}\le p^*_{\mathrm{SSD},U}\le p^*_{\mathrm{FSD},U}\). First-order dominance is stricter than second-order, so each FSD threshold sits outside the matching SSD threshold. In the common-cost/common-payoff case (\(C=C^*,\,X=X^*\)) these reduce to \(p^*_{\mathrm{FSD},L}=\mathbb{P}(\overline I_N=1)\), \(p^*_{\mathrm{SSD},L}=p\), and \(p^*_{\mathrm{SSD},U}=p^*_{\mathrm{FSD},U}=1-\mathbb{P}(\overline I_N=0)\). When costs or payoffs differ (\(C\neq C^*\) or \(X\neq X^*\)) they are instead governed by \(Y=(C^*-C+X\overline I_N)/X^*\). The Kelly/log threshold \(p^*_{\log}\) always lies in the no-dominance middle.

Reading the calculator: a focused asset wins a criterion when its input \(p^*\) exceeds that criterion's break-even \(p^*\) threshold. The thresholds are not meant to agree with each other: expected NPV is a mean-value criterion, loss probability is a downside-tail criterion, Sharpe is a mean-risk criterion, and log utility is preference based.
